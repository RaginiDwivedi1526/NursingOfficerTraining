Accurate recognition of the client's symptoms is imperative prior to developing solutions and
implementing interventions. Common disease processes presenting with similar symptoms
related to sympathetic nervous system activation include:

e Hyperthyroidism: A hypermetabolic state due to thyroid hormone overproduction,
hyperthyroidism is associated with upregulation of beta-adrenergic receptors and
increased adrenergic tone. Associated clinical features include diaphoresis, tremor,
trembling hands, heart palpitations, weight loss despite increased appetite,
diarrhea, and shortness of breath. Hyperthyroidism can lead to high-output heart
failure due to hyperdynamic circulation.

e Myocardial infarction: Reduced blood flow through the coronary arteries (eg, from
atherosclerosis, vasospasm) may induce myocardial ischemia that can progress to
myocardial infarction (ie, tissue death) without prompt intervention. Clinical
manifestations include heart palpitations, chest pain, shortness of breath, and
diaphoresis. Trembling of the hands is not a sign of a myocardial infarction.

e Panic disorder: Recurrent, brief (eg, 10- to 30-min) episodes in which a client
experiences sudden, intense anxiety; fear; and physiologic discomfort occurs as a
result of an imbalance of the neurotransmitters norepinephrine and gamma-
aminobutyric acid (GABA). These may or may not occur in response to an obvious
stressor. Symptoms resemble many cardiac and respiratory conditions and include a
fear of dying, heart palpitations, chest pain, shortness of breath, trembling hands,
and diaphoresis.

Clients with panic disorder are often treated with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors
(SSRIs) (eg, sertraline) or selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) (eg,
venlafaxine) for long-term maintenance. During the initial stages, anxiety may worsen
because clients with panic disorder are quite sensitive to these medications. Therefore,
benzodiazepines are frequently co prescribed in the first few weeks of treatment because
they work much faster and are more efficacious.

Benzodiazepines (eg, lorazepam, alprazolam) are central nervous system (CNS)
depressants that work by potentiating the effect of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA).
GABA is a powerful inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain that decreases the excitability of
neurons, producing a sedative effect.

Clients should avoid driving while taking benzodiazepines because these medications can
cause fatigue, drowsiness, and sedation, especially during the initiation of therapy and
following dosage increases (Option 1). Abrupt withdrawal of benzodiazepines can
precipitate potentially dangerous withdrawal symptoms (eg, seizures) (Option 3). A21-
year-old client has an increased risk of experiencing suicidal thoughts when starting
sertraline; the client should be instructed to notify the health care provider immediately if
suicidal thoughts occur (Option 2).

(Option 4) Clients should avoid taking benzodiazepines (eg, alprazolam, clonazepam) with

mood (eg, depression, sadness) or loss of interest in daily activities. Additional symptoms
include feelings of worthlessness, poor concentration, and thoughts of self-harm/suicide.
Changes in appetite and sleep patterns can lead to weight fluctuations (eg, weight loss or
gain) and sleep disturbances.

The nurse should assess for thoughts of self-harm and perform a suicide assessment.
Creating a safety plan can help the client identify coping skills, a support system, and
warning signs of an impending crisis (eg, changes in mood, thoughts, or behavior).
Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (eg, escitalopram) are commonly used to treat MDD
and work by increasing the availability of serotonin in the brain. Clients should be monitored
for the presence of a suicide plan and sudden improvement in mood, which can
indicate an impending suicide attempt.

disorder. It has a long half-life, which allows it to effectively suppress cravings and
withdrawal symptoms. It also blocks the euphoric effects of other opioids by maintaining high
tolerance levels. The client who is prescribed methadone should:

e Avoid alcohol and other central nervous system depressants (eg, benzodiazepines) to
prevent respiratory depression (Option 1)

e Rise slowly to a standing position because orthostatic hypotension can occur
(Option 2)

e Notify the health care provider immediately of symptoms of cardiac arrhythmias (eg,
dizziness, heart palpitations) because methadone can cause QT-interval
prolongation and lethal arrhythmias (eg, torsade de pointes) (Option 4)

¢ Increase intake of water and fiber to prevent constipation

(Option 3) Clients can easily overdose on methadone if they take additional doses because
the medication remains active in the system after its effects wear off. The client should notify
the health care provider if additional treatment is necessary to suppress cravings.

(Option 5) Like other opioids, methadone produces dependence, and abrupt discontinuation
causes withdrawal symptoms. The nurse should provide teaching regarding expected
adverse effects of the medication (eg, constipation) and offer guidance for coping with these
effects.

Educational objective:

Methadone is a medication used for withdrawal and maintenance treatment for opioid use
disorder. Clients prescribed methadone should avoid alcohol, rise slowly to a standing
position, and notify the health care provider of symptoms of cardiac arrhythmias.

Treatment for clients with posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) includes trauma-focused
cognitive behavioral therapy and antidepressant medications (eg, selective serotonin
reuptake inhibitors). When caring for clients with PTSD, the nurse should anticipate
interventions that will promote safety, reduce stress, improve anxiety, and encourage
relaxation. Appropriate interventions include:
e Assisting the client to identify available support systems to assist in long-term
recovery
e Directly asking if the client is having thoughts of self-harm due to the high risk for
suicide in clients with PTSD
e Assessing the client's ability to perform activities of daily living because PTSD
can cause significant interpersonal dysfunction
e Teaching the client to use progressive muscle relaxation to provide the client with a
way to independently manage onset of anxiety
It is not appropriate to provide the client with privacy during flashbacks. The nurse
should remain with the client to offer reassurance of safety to calm anxiety.
It is not appropriate for the nurse to avoid discussion of the precipitating event when
speaking to the client. Debriefing is the first step toward resolution of PTSD.
Educational objective:
When caring for a client with posttraumatic stress disorder, the nurse should assist the client
to identify available support systems, directly ask if the client is having thoughts of self-harm,
assess the client's ability to perform activities of daily living, and teach the client to use
progressive muscle relaxation to decrease anxiety.

suicidal ideation include:
e Assigning the client to a shared room near the nurses' station to reduce social
isolation and allow easier access to the client.
¢ Avoiding utensils on the client's meal tray that could be used for self-harm.
e Checking on the client at frequent, irregular intervals (if not under 1-to-1
observation) to lessen predictability of staff surveillance.
e Performing frequent room searches for harmful objects to ensure client safety.
e Performing mouth checks after medication administration to ensure the client has
swallowed medication and is not saving them for a future overdose attempt.
e Encouraging the client to participate in grooming and hygiene as the client may
exhibit loss of interest in daily activities, decreased energy, and lack of motivation.

check when the client is using the restroom. The nurse must ensure that there is visual
contact with the client during safety checks, even if the client is in the restroom, to ensure
safety.

establish a nonjudgmental, therapeutic relationship that allows for open communication.
