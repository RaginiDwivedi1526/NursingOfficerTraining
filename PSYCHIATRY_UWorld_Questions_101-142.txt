Serotonin syndrome (ie, serotonin toxicity) is a life-threatening condition caused by excess
serotonin in the central nervous system. Serotonin syndrome can occur when a client starts
taking or increases the dose of a serotonergic medication (eg, selective serotonin reuptake
inhibitors [eg, paroxetine], tricyclic antidepressants) or is due to medication interactions.
Clinical manifestations can appear within several hours and include:

* Altered mental status (eg, agitation, confusion, hallucinations)

+ Neuromuscular excitement (eq, jitters, tremors, shivering)

+ Autonomic symptoms (eg, high fever, diaphoresis, diarrhea)
Serotonin syndrome typically resolves after discontinuation of the serotonergic medication.
The nurse should prioritize a client with suspected serotonin syndrome because, without
prompt intervention (eg, cooling blanket, mechanical ventilation), symptoms may progress to
cardiovascular shock, seizures, or death (Option 2).
(Option 1) Alprazolam is a benzodiazepine used to treat anxiety and panic disorders.
Weakness and dizziness are common side effects and are not life-threatening.
(Option 3) Olanzapine is an atypical antipsychotic used to treat schizophrenia, bipolar
disorder, and other mental health disorders. Fatigue and metabolic effects (eg, weight gain,
hyperglycemia) are common side effects that do not require immediate intervention.
(Option 4) Imipramine is a tricyclic antidepressant used to treat major depressive disorder.
Insomnia and anticholinergic side effects (eg, dry mouth, constipation) are common and
typically subside with time or treatment.
Educational objective:
Serotonin syndrome (ie, serotonin toxicity) is a life-threatening condition caused by excess
serotonin in the central nervous system. Without prompt intervention (eg, cooling blanket,
mechanical ventilation), symptoms may progress to cardiovascular shock, seizures, or death.

Methadone is a medication used for withdrawal and maintenance treatment for opioid use
disorder. It has a long half-life, which allows it to effectively suppress cravings and
withdrawal symptoms. It also blocks the euphoric effects of other opioids by maintaining high
tolerance levels. The client who is prescribed methadone should:

e Avoid alcohol and other central nervous system depressants (eg, benzodiazepines) to
prevent respiratory depression (Option 1)

e Rise slowly to a standing position because orthostatic hypotension can occur
(Option 2)

e Notify the health care provider immediately of symptoms of cardiac arrhythmias (eg,
dizziness, heart palpitations) because methadone can cause QT-interval
prolongation and lethal arrhythmias (eg, torsade de pointes) (Option 4)

e Increase intake of water and fiber to prevent constipation

(Option 3) Clients can easily overdose on methadone if they take additional doses because
the medication remains active in the system after its effects wear off. The client should notify
the health care provider if additional treatment is necessary to suppress cravings.

(Option 5) Like other opioids, methadone produces dependence, and abrupt discontinuation
causes withdrawal symptoms. The nurse should provide teaching regarding expected
adverse effects of the medication (eg, constipation) and offer guidance for coping with these
effects.

Educational objective:

Methadone is a medication used for withdrawal and maintenance treatment for opioid use
disorder. Clients prescribed methadone should avoid alcohol, rise slowly to a standing
position, and notify the health care provider of symptoms of cardiac arrhythmias.
